Activities

This page documents the formative learning activities completed during the Intelligent Agents module. Each activity summarises the task focus, what I completed, the evidence produced, and the learning outcomes achieved. Together, these activities demonstrate progressive understanding of agent-based systems, communication, ethics, and applied intelligent technologies.

Formative activities Practical artefacts Evidence-based learning

Activity 1: Agent Concepts and Architectures

Focus: core concepts of intelligent agents, agent environments, and architectural models.

What I did Evidence + learning
  • Reviewed definitions of intelligent agents, autonomy, rationality, and agent functions.
  • Analysed environment properties such as observability, determinism, and episodic vs sequential tasks.
  • Compared reactive, deliberative, hybrid, and BDI architectures and evaluated their strengths and weaknesses.
Evidence
Conceptual notes and application of architectures in Collaborative Discussion 1 and reflections.
Outcome
Ability to critically select appropriate agent architectures based on task complexity and operational constraints.

References

  • Russell, S. and Norvig, P. (2021) Artificial Intelligence: A Modern Approach. 4th edn. Harlow: Pearson.
  • Wooldridge, M. (2021) An Introduction to MultiAgent Systems. 2nd edn. Chichester: Wiley.
  • Rao, A.S. and Georgeff, M.P. (1995) ‘BDI agents: From theory to practice’, ICMAS, pp. 312–319.

Activity 2: Multi-Agent Coordination and Emergent Behaviour

Focus: coordination mechanisms and emergent behaviour in decentralised systems.

What I did Evidence + learning
  • Explored coordination strategies including communication, negotiation, and shared protocols.
  • Analysed how local agent decisions can lead to unintended global behaviour.
  • Connected emergent risks to governance and accountability requirements.
Evidence
Collaborative Discussion 1 (initial post, peer responses, and summary).
Outcome
Improved ability to evaluate MAS as socio-technical systems rather than purely technical artefacts.

References

  • Macal, C.M. and North, M.J. (2010) ‘Tutorial on agent-based modelling and simulation’, Journal of Simulation, 4(3), pp. 151–162.
  • Rahwan, I. (2018) ‘Society-in-the-loop’, Ethics and Information Technology, 20(1), pp. 5–14.

Activity 3: Agent Communication and ACLs (KQML / FIPA-ACL)

Focus: agent communication languages and comparison with method invocation.

What I did Evidence + learning
  • Studied speech acts and communicative intent in agent interaction.
  • Compared ACLs with Python/Java method invocation.
  • Evaluated trade-offs between semantic expressiveness and computational overhead.
Evidence
Collaborative Discussion 2 (initial post, peer responses, and summary).
Outcome
Clear understanding of when ACLs are essential for autonomy and when method invocation is preferable.

References

  • Finin, T. et al. (1994) ‘KQML as an agent communication language’, CIKM, pp. 456–463.
  • Gruber, T. (1993) ‘A translation approach to portable ontology specifications’, Knowledge Acquisition, 5(2), pp. 199–220.
  • Weiss, G. (1999) Multiagent Systems. MIT Press.

Activity 4: Ethics and Risk in Autonomous Systems

Focus: ethical and governance risks associated with intelligent and autonomous systems.

What I did Evidence + learning
  • Examined risks including bias, accountability gaps, and unintended consequences.
  • Connected ethical concerns to real-world deployment contexts.
  • Applied responsible AI frameworks to evaluate system behaviour.
Evidence
Collaborative Discussion 3 contributions and reflective synthesis.
Outcome
Ability to assess intelligent systems using ethical and governance criteria alongside technical performance.

References

  • Dignum, V. (2019) Responsible Artificial Intelligence. Springer.
  • Floridi, L. et al. (2018) ‘AI4People’, Minds and Machines, 28(4), pp. 689–707.

Activity 5: Applying Intelligent Agents to My Professional Context

Focus: mapping agent concepts to realistic organisational and operational environments.

What I did Evidence + learning
  • Identified operational tasks suitable for agent support.
  • Outlined governance, auditability, and accountability requirements.
  • Reflected on human-in-the-loop decision-making.
Evidence
Integrated reflections and discussion synthesis.
Outcome
Stronger ability to justify agent deployment based on context and risk.

Activity 8: Deep Learning in Action

Focus: ethical and socio-technical implications of deep learning–enabled generative technologies.

What I did Evidence + learning

I analysed deep learning–enabled generative technologies such as ChatGPT and DALL·E, focusing on ethical risks including bias and fairness, misinformation, intellectual property, and transparency. I argued that these risks are structural outcomes of large-scale training data and opaque model behaviour, and that responsible deployment requires governance frameworks, auditing, and human oversight.

Evidence
Collaborative Discussion 3 (Units 9–11): initial post, peer responses, and summary post.
Outcome
Ability to critically evaluate deep learning systems beyond performance metrics.

References

  • Bolukbasi, T. et al. (2016) ‘Debiasing word embeddings’, NeurIPS, pp. 4349–4357.
  • Chesney, R. and Citron, D. (2019) ‘Deepfakes’, California Law Review, 107(6), pp. 1753–1820.
  • Floridi, L. et al. (2018) ‘AI4People’, Minds and Machines, 28(4), pp. 689–707.
