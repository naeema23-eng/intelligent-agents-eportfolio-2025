Final Reflective Account

This reflective account evaluates my learning, individual contributions, and professional development throughout the Intelligent Agents module. The reflection follows the structured approach proposed by Rolfe et al. (2001): What? – So What? – Now What?

Reflection Critical analysis Unit 6 evaluation

What? — Context, activities, and my role

The Intelligent Agents module explored the theory, design, and evaluation of agent-based and multi-agent systems, integrating conceptual foundations with applied and collaborative learning. Across Units 1–12, I participated in three structured collaborative discussions, analytical peer responses, applied reasoning activities, and a development team project in Unit 6. Together, these components formed a coherent learning journey that combined technical understanding, academic debate, and reflective practice.

The most substantial applied component was the Unit 6 Development Team Project, in which our group designed a multi-agent system (MAS) for digital forensic triage. The proposed system decomposed a forensic workflow into specialised agents responsible for file scanning, filtering, hashing, secure archiving, and controlled transmission. Design decisions were informed by forensic constraints such as chain of custody, evidential integrity, and auditability, and were conceptually aligned with agent-oriented principles including autonomy, modularity, and controlled interaction (Wooldridge, 2021).

My formally agreed role within the team was Project Lead, as documented in the Team Contract. I was responsible for coordinating timelines, overseeing the system architecture, integrating individual contributions, and ensuring alignment with the assessment criteria. I also contributed directly to the architectural design narrative, the explanation of agent roles and workflow, and sections of the critical evaluation. In addition, I took responsibility for final integration, academic tone, and consistency of referencing across the report.

Alongside the team project, I actively engaged in collaborative discussions throughout the module. These required producing literature-grounded initial posts, responding critically to peers, and synthesising learning in summary posts. Discussion themes included agent-based systems in organisations, agent communication languages, and ethical challenges associated with learning-enabled and generative intelligent systems.

So What? — Critical analysis, emotions, and learning

At the beginning of the module, my understanding of intelligent agents was primarily technical. I viewed agents mainly as architectural components designed to improve scalability, decentralisation, and performance. While this perspective provided a solid foundation, engagement with peer discussion, academic literature, and especially the Unit 6 project revealed important limitations in this approach.

The Unit 6 team project marked a turning point in my learning. Designing a MAS for digital forensics made clear that agent-based systems are socio-technical systems, operating within legal, ethical, and organisational constraints rather than purely computational environments. Forensic requirements such as evidential admissibility and traceability significantly shaped design choices. Although our submission demonstrated strong domain understanding and a coherent pipeline, the tutor feedback highlighted a key weakness: several design decisions were described rather than critically justified in relation to operational constraints.

This feedback was particularly instructive. It revealed a tendency in my own work to rely on broadly accepted best practices—such as modular pipelines, hashing, and agent separation—without always interrogating why a specific choice was optimal in a given context. This highlighted the distinction between competent explanation and postgraduate-level critical evaluation, where alternatives must be weighed explicitly.

Emotionally, leading the team was both demanding and formative. I experienced a strong sense of responsibility for integration quality and deadlines, particularly when managing different writing styles and technical emphases. While this pressure was challenging, it reinforced the importance of early risk identification, clear role definition, and disciplined communication.

The collaborative discussions further reshaped my thinking. Discussion 1 challenged my optimism about agent autonomy by exposing risks of emergent behaviour under weak governance. Discussion 2 clarified the trade-offs involved in agent communication languages, while Discussion 3 reframed ethics from a compliance requirement into a core engineering responsibility (Floridi et al., 2018; Dignum, 2019).

Now What? — Changed actions, skills gained, and future application

As a result of this module, my approach to designing and evaluating intelligent agent systems has fundamentally changed. I now assess agent-based solutions across four integrated dimensions: technical feasibility, domain constraints, ethical risk, and governance readiness.

In future projects, I will explicitly compare alternative architectures and justify design decisions against real operational constraints. This directly addresses the weaknesses identified in the Unit 6 feedback and represents a clear change in my analytical practice.

Acting as Project Lead strengthened my ability to coordinate collaborative work, manage academic quality, and integrate diverse contributions into a coherent artefact. Structured peer discussion improved my academic communication, confidence in critique, and ability to revise positions based on evidence.

Finally, this module clarified my long-term academic direction. I am particularly interested in intelligent systems that support human decision-making rather than replace it, and in agent-based approaches that balance autonomy with accountability (Russell and Norvig, 2021; Wooldridge, 2021).

Conclusion

Overall, the Intelligent Agents module provided a demanding and transformative learning experience. Through collaborative discussion, applied system design, and structured reflection, I developed a more mature understanding of intelligent agents as embedded, governable, and evaluable systems rather than isolated technical solutions.
