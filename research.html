Research Awareness & Literature Engagement

This page is not a full research proposal. It documents the key academic ideas, theories, and literature that informed my discussions, practical activities, and design decisions throughout the Intelligent Agents module.

Literature Research awareness Academic grounding

1. Key research themes explored

1.1 Defining intelligent agents

A core theme throughout the module was understanding what constitutes an intelligent agent and how agents differ from traditional software components. I drew on foundational definitions of autonomy, rationality, reactivity, proactiveness, and social ability to frame both theoretical discussion and implementation choices (Russell and Norvig, 2021; Wooldridge, 2009).

1.2 Coordination and interaction in multi-agent systems

My engagement with multi-agent systems focused on how independent agents cooperate or compete under distributed control and limited information. Research on coordination mechanisms, negotiation, and task allocation helped me reason about robustness, scalability, and emergent behaviour in realistic systems (Jennings, 2000; Ferber, 1999).

1.3 Architectures and development frameworks

Bridging theory and practice required understanding agent architectures and development frameworks. I examined how conceptual models such as BDI and hybrid architectures are realised in practice through platforms such as JADE and FIPA-aligned communication, informing my design justifications and system explanations (Bellifemine, Caire and Greenwood, 2007; Wooldridge, 2009).

1.4 Evaluation, limitations, and ethics

Evaluation was treated as more than performance measurement. I considered system assumptions, failure modes, and ethical implications including bias, manipulation, and unsafe automation. This research lens influenced my emphasis on governance, transparency, and accountability in agent-based systems (Floridi et al., 2018; Russell and Norvig, 2021).

2. Selected literature and its relevance

  1. Wooldridge (2009) — Core reference for agent definitions, architectures, and multi-agent reasoning. Used as a conceptual anchor across the module.
  2. Russell and Norvig (2021) — Provided broader AI context, particularly rational agents, environments, and decision-making under uncertainty.
  3. Jennings (2000) — Supported arguments for agent-oriented approaches in complex, distributed systems.
  4. Ferber (1999) — Clarified coordination challenges and emergent behaviour in MAS.
  5. Bellifemine, Caire and Greenwood (2007) — Offered practical insight into agent implementation using JADE and FIPA standards.
  6. Shoham and Leyton-Brown (2009) — Introduced game-theoretic and incentive-based perspectives on agent interaction.
  7. Nwana (1996) — Provided an accessible taxonomy of agent types and applications.
  8. Floridi et al. (2018) — Framed ethical and governance considerations for responsible AI and agent systems.

3. How research informed my module work

3.1 Discussions

Academic literature enabled me to structure discussion posts analytically rather than descriptively. Concepts such as agent rationality, environment assumptions, and coordination mechanisms supported more critical engagement with peer contributions (Russell and Norvig, 2021; Jennings, 2000).

3.2 Activities and practical tasks

Research on agent architectures and development frameworks informed how I interpreted implementation choices during practical tasks, allowing me to justify designs using academic language rather than purely technical description (Wooldridge, 2009; Bellifemine et al., 2007).

3.3 Project design decisions

  • Autonomy vs. control: balancing independent agent decision-making with governance and human oversight (Wooldridge, 2009; Floridi et al., 2018).
  • Coordination complexity: selecting interaction mechanisms that scale while reducing failure risk (Jennings, 2000; Ferber, 1999).
  • Evaluation realism: aligning metrics and assumptions with the intended operating environment (Russell and Norvig, 2021).

4. Forward-looking research interests

Building on this module, I am interested in applying agent-based approaches in healthcare and emergency response contexts, where coordination, uncertainty, and safety constraints are critical.

  • Agent-assisted decision support under uncertainty and time pressure.
  • Multi-agent coordination for distributed operational environments.
  • Responsible agent systems with transparency, auditability, and human oversight.

These interests align closely with my professional context and provide a foundation for future MSc project work.
